The Core Dose Equation

The committed equivalent dose to a target tissue (H50,T) is the culmination of understanding how radionuclides move through the body and deposit energy. This section introduces the main formula and its key components.

H50,T = (Constant) × ΣS [ US × SEE(T ← S) ]

Why is Dose Derivation Important?

Understanding dose derivation provides the "why" behind the numbers in dose tables and software like IMBA. It connects the conceptual models of radionuclide behavior to the quantitative assessment of radiation risk.

US Breakdown: Ultimate Number of Disintegrations

US quantifies the total radioactive decay that occurs within a specific source organ over the 50-year commitment period from a 1 Bq intake. It's essentially the integral of activity over time.

Conceptual Calculation

For a single compartment with exponential activity decrease ($A(t) = A(0)e^{-kt}$), US is found by integrating this function over 50 years. For linear (catenary) models, the "Skrable's Dragon" concept simplifies this: if removal is fast, all activity entering a compartment will eventually decay or clear within 50 years.

Key Idea: US tells us *how long* a radionuclide stays in an organ and *how much* it decays there. More disintegrations mean higher potential dose.

SEE Breakdown: Specific Effective Energy

SEE(T ← S) quantifies the energy deposited in a target organ (T) per unit mass, for each nuclear transformation occurring in a source organ (S).

Components of SEE

The calculation of SEE considers several factors:

  • Y (Yield): How many particles of a specific type (alpha, beta, gamma) are emitted per transformation.
  • E (Energy): The average energy of that particle type (in MeV).
  • AF (Absorbed Fraction): The fraction of energy emitted in the source organ that is actually absorbed in the target organ. (Often 1 for alpha/beta in the source organ, more complex for gamma).
  • Q (Quality Factor): The radiation weighting factor, accounting for biological effectiveness (e.g., 1 for photons/electrons, 20 for alpha).
  • M (Mass of Target Organ): The mass of the target organ (in grams), used to normalize energy deposition.

Key Idea: SEE tells us *where* the energy goes and *how effective* it is at causing damage in specific target tissues.

Cobalt-60 Example: Putting it All Together

Let's walk through the conceptual steps of calculating the dose coefficient for a 1 Bq intake of Class Y Cobalt-60 (60Co), as discussed in Lecture 4. This illustrates how US and SEE combine.

1. Initial Atoms and US Calculation

For 1 Bq of 60Co (half-life ~5.271 years), the initial number of atoms is approximately 2.4 × 108. This activity then distributes through the respiratory, GI, and systemic models. US is calculated for each compartment based on deposition, transfer rates, and decay.

For example, for the lungs, after considering deposition and clearance, the total disintegrations (US) would be a significant value due to the long retention of Class Y material.

2. SEE Factor Determination

60Co is a beta-minus emitter followed by two high-energy gamma rays. For each disintegration, we determine how much energy is deposited in various target organs. For instance:

  • Beta energy from 60Co decaying in the lungs is largely absorbed *within* the lungs (AF ≈ 1).
  • Gamma energy from 60Co decaying in the lungs can travel and deposit energy in distant organs (e.g., liver, total body), meaning AF < 1.

3. Combining US and SEE for Dose

The US for each source organ is multiplied by the corresponding SEE values for all target organs. These contributions are summed up. For example, the total committed equivalent dose to the lung would include contributions from 60Co decaying in the lung itself, the GI tract, and systemic organs.

4. Final Committed Effective Dose

After calculating the committed equivalent dose for all target tissues, these are multiplied by their respective tissue weighting factors (wT) and summed to yield the total Committed Effective Dose Equivalent (CEDE).

For 60Co, the final calculated dose conversion factors (DCFs) are:

  • ICRP 30 rule: ~8.89 × 10-9 Sv/Bq
  • ICRP 30 supplement rule: ~7.64 × 10-9 Sv/Bq

This demonstrates how the complex interplay of radionuclide behavior and energy deposition culminates in a single dose coefficient value.
